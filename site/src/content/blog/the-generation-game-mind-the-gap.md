It's no secret that a seismic demographic shift is underway across the trades in the Built Environment.

There's a well-documented and increasingly concerning skills gap between the time-served, over-50s; plumbers, decorators, electricians, fitters who stand by brand loyalty, long-standing supplier relationships and bulk deals - and 'Generation Toolbelt'; an emerging wave of digitally native, Gen Z tradespeople who are much more open to experimentation, driven by peer reviews and responsive deliveries. For product manufacturers, it's both a challenge and an opportunity. How do you position your brand to resonate with these two different mindsets?

**Why the gap?** A perfect storm of demographic shifts, cultural perception of trades, policy failure, and industry inertia has meant a whole generation being nudged away from trades, creating the huge vacuum in trade talent we see today. The fight is all too real when your most closely guarded secret is your electrician's phone number. Or you're willing to wait two years for 'your' plumber to tile the en-suite.

According to Checkatrade's UK Trade Skills Index report, 35% of construction workers are over 50. When retirement is both sudden and imminent, the industry will soon lose up to a third of its skilled labour - along with decades of knowledge and experience, in one generation. The report highlighted that the home improvement and repair sector will grow by 40% over the next decade and that the UK would need to find 1.3 million new skilled tradespeople and over 350,000 new apprentices to deliver the Government's housing and net zero targets.

But all is not lost. Gen Z are starting to form an orderly queue for a career in the trades. As the B&Q The Way We Live Now report points out, there is a rise in younger individuals, motivated by factors like financial independence, job flexibility and the avoidance of student debt. Young people are also increasingly starting to see trades as future-proof careers that AI can't easily replace.

**The over-50s: brand loyalists** — They've built their careers on trust and reliability. They know what works, and they stick with it. Their buying habits are based on recognition, trust and consistency. Supplier relationships are personal and long-term; promotions that reward bulk purchases and long-term planning resonate.

**'Generation Toolbelt': the digital natives** — They're entrepreneurial, tech-savvy and community-driven. Not afraid to try something new, based on innovation or peer buzz. You'll only get one chance to deliver on your bold claims, but if you capture their imagination, they'll put it out on their socials. They shop around, pay attention to reviews and trust their community. Orders are on-demand, delivered to the job, so bulk has no pull.

**Bridging the gap** — When you choose the right tool, your brand can speak to both:
1. **Pride in craft and competence** — Both value skill, mastery and pride in doing the job right. Layer your language to celebrate craftsmanship; tap into professional standards (respect/reputation for older; credibility/progress for younger).
2. **Tools, tech and training** — Ease, efficiency and time-saving tools are appreciated across both. Position tech as a tool to make their job easier, not a threat. Avoid over-hyping innovation without real benefit.
3. **Community and recognition** — Both respond to community content showing achievements and challenges ('Pro of the Month', 'Tools of the Trade'). Older skews Facebook/YouTube; Gen Z skews TikTok/Insta.
4. **Trust in the brand** — Trust, quality and reliability remain top buying drivers. Trade endorsements, reviews, performance tests, durability metrics and confident warranties build the trust factor. Invest in real-job photography, not over-polished stock.
5. **Safety, health and wellbeing** — Older tradespeople think long-term wear and tear; Gen Z care about mental health, burnout and balance. Build emotional intelligence and genuine empathy into your brand.

**Brands that build bridges** find commonality in deep-seated human principles: Pride in the craft (identity-based) · Tools that work smarter (results-driven) · Community (peer recognition) · Trustworthy brand (consistency, performance) · Wellbeing and safety (empathy).
