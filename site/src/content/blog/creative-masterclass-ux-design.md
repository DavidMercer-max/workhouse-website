As design challenges become more complex, so does the need to align to user needs and expectations. The pandemic accelerated our need to work together collaboratively. When designing any digital experience, put the end-user at the heart — but go beyond standard research and an 'us and them' approach. **'Co-design'** is a collaborative methodology that digs into the perspectives of internal and external stakeholders to co-create experiences that create equal value for company and customer.

Break UX down into three parts: **UR (User Research), UX (User Experience Design) and UI (User Interface Design).**

**Starting with UR** — Involve people with different perspectives early and regularly. The objective is to capture and translate user insights into meaningful experiences — starting by identifying core user groups including, crucially, sceptics, brand 'passengers' and advocates. Don't underestimate internal stakeholders (frontline teams help identify the right users to research). A dedicated User Researcher streamlines recruitment. Principles: *Go wide* (find clarity on core groups; insights from non-subscribers matter as much as fans) · *Be prepared to get uncomfortable* (avoid assumptions, ask open-ended questions, ask 'why' not 'what/how') · *Share and test findings early* (be iterative; don't wait for 'ta-dah' moments).

**Moving forward with UX** — Take research findings, plus business and brand strategy, and develop a sitemap and information architecture — collaboratively with client and users. Low-fidelity prototyping on key tasks keeps structure user-centred. **Producing clickable wireframe prototypes is an efficient testing mechanism that later translates into high-level design concepts** — letting users be objective about the task without creative distractions, and letting the UX designer address changes live and re-test efficiently.

**Connecting the creative dots with UI** — The most subjective stage: interface your brand's distinctive assets with the user journey. Investment in understanding user motivations pays dividends here. Brand guidelines aren't always fit for digital — colours, fonts and styles don't always translate, causing accessibility issues that must be flushed out. Test and validate with core users until you find the balance of stimulating creativity and frictionless functionality.

**Playing the long game** — User testing should be permanent; once launched, every website needs a continual improvement process to stay a true reflection of the brand and chime with the changing needs of the audience.
