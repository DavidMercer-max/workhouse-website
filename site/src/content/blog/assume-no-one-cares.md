**What Is the 95:5 Rule?** Roughly only 5% of your target audience are actively looking to buy at any given time. The other 95% are out of market. Not ready. But they will be, eventually — and when they are, you want your brand right there, top of mind. The concept was developed by Professor John Dawes at the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute and popularised by LinkedIn's B2B Institute. Its implications are huge for the Built Environment, where purchase cycles are long and infrequent and buying is complex with big-ticket items.

**Chasing the 5%** — Most marketing in the Built Environment is geared to short-term wins; lead gen, promotions, conversion campaigns. But focusing solely on the 5% who are ready to buy is like fishing in a puddle when there's an ocean nearby. The real opportunity lies in the 95% who will need you someday. The challenge: making sure they think of you when they do.

**Where is everybody?** — Most of your future revenue comes from buyers who aren't ready today. In construction, engineering or materials supply there are numerous stakeholders — architects, contractors, installers, surveyors and procurement — all with different priorities and timelines. If you're not already on the mental shortlist when they buy, you're playing catch-up.

**Building Mental Availability** — Coined by Professor Byron Sharp and Jenni Romaniuk (Ehrenberg-Bass). Mental availability isn't just awareness — it's being the first name that comes to mind in a buying situation. Key principles:
- **Read the Room** — Differentiation is critical: be *meaningfully* different, owning an idea, tone or perspective that resonates. Remove the 'brand manager blinkers' and see the world through the customer's eyes.
- **Explore Deeper Connections** — Emotional connections linger long after rational arguments fade. B2B doesn't have to be dry — in fact it should never be. Brands that refuse to play it safe break through and command premium pricing.
- **Build Fluency** — Distinctive brand assets (logo, typeface, colour palette, endline, product shape, jingle) are critical mental shortcuts — the 'do they know it's me?' test. Think golden arches, the meerkat, the Netflix 'da-dum'. Underestimated and underutilised in B2B.
- **Learn to Play the Long Game** — Consistency builds memory. Repetition isn't boring, it's a strategy. Hammer home a single simple message (durability, innovation, sustainability) until it's synonymous with your brand.
- **Find Balance — the Long AND Short of it** — You can't choose between brand building and sales activation; you need both (see Binet & Field, 'The Long & the Short of it'). Brands that grow consistently master both: memorable enough to be chosen later, visible enough to be bought now.

Take a fresh look at your brand. Assume no-one cares. How would you build a brand that lasts?
